Galileo Galilei lived in Italy during the Renaissance. He was born on 15 February 1564 in the city of Pisa, Italy, and he died on 8 January 1642 in the city of Arcetri, Italy. He was a famous physicist and astronomer.

As a physicist, he worked with force and acceleration, and pioneered the scientific method. The scientific method is the foundation of modern science. Using the scientific method, scientific knowledge are obtained by experimentation and observation. As an astronomer, he invented the telescope, and used it to discover many astronomical bodies, including moons of Jupiter.

Galileo used his inclined plane experiment to obtain the constant acceleration of objects in free fall.

Because the speeds of falling objects were too fast to be measured in Galileo's days, he rolled balls down inclined planes and recorded the relation between the distances the balls traveled and the times the balls took. After many experimental trials, he found that, for a ball traveling down an inclined plane, doubling the time it travels results in four times the distance it travels. Objects in free fall accelerate at a constant rate. This rate is the constant g, having a value of about 9.8 m/s2.

Galileo used his leaning tower of Pisa experiment to demonstrate that heavier objects do not necessarily fall faster than lighter objects.

The ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle theorized that the heavier an object is, the faster it falls, and vice versa. For millenia, this was believed to be true. In 1589, Galileo did an experiment to test Aristotle's theory by dropping two balls from the top of the leaning tower of Pisa. The two balls had the same shape and material, except one was heavier and the other was lighter. Galileo dropped both balls at the same time, and both balls landed on the ground at the same time. He demonstrated that the speed at which an object falls is not related to its weight.

As an astronomer, Galileo invented the telescope. He used it to make remarkable astronomical discoveries.

In 1608, the spyglass, which magnifies an object by a few times, appeared in Europe. Galileo's telescope, invented in 1610, consists of a hollow cylinder with a spyglass at both ends, one convex and the other concave. Galileo's telescope can magnify a distant object by 30 times.

Using his telescope, Galileo observed that the Moon has a rough surface rather than a smooth surface that people once believed, and he discovered the four largest moons of Jupiter that are now known as the Galilean moons. His observations of the phases of Venus provided evidence for the heliocentric theory proposed by Nicolaus Copernicus in 1543.
